# San Francisco Affordable Housing Initiative 2025

## Executive Summary

This policy proposes a comprehensive affordable housing program for San Francisco to address the city's housing crisis through zoning reforms, financial incentives, and public-private partnerships.

---

## Policy Objectives

1. **Increase Affordable Housing Supply**
   - Build 10,000 new affordable housing units within 5 years
   - Convert 20% of new developments to affordable units
   - Preserve existing affordable housing stock

2. **Reduce Housing Costs**
   - Cap rent increases at 3% annually
   - Provide down payment assistance for first-time homebuyers
   - Offer tax credits to developers building affordable units

3. **Improve Housing Access**
   - Streamline permitting process for affordable housing projects
   - Prioritize transit-oriented development
   - Ensure geographic distribution across all neighborhoods

---

## Key Policy Actions

### Action 1: Upzone High-Opportunity Areas
**Description:** Rezone single-family neighborhoods near transit to allow multi-family housing

**Implementation:**
- Identify areas within 0.5 miles of major transit stations
- Allow up to 6-story buildings in these zones
- Require 25% of units to be affordable (80% AMI or below)
- Fast-track permitting for qualifying projects

**Expected Impact:**
- 15,000 new housing units over 10 years
- Reduced commute times by 12%
- Increased tax revenue by $50M annually

### Action 2: Developer Inclusionary Requirements
**Description:** Mandate affordable unit inclusion in all new residential developments

**Implementation:**
- Require 20% affordable units in buildings over 10 units
- Offer density bonuses for exceeding requirements (up to 30% affordable)
- Allow off-site construction or fee payment as alternatives
- Implement strict monitoring and compliance

**Expected Impact:**
- 2,500 affordable units annually
- Mixed-income neighborhoods
- Reduced displacement

### Action 3: Public Land Development Program
**Description:** Develop affordable housing on publicly owned land

**Implementation:**
- Identify 50 underutilized public parcels
- Partner with affordable housing developers
- Provide land at below-market rates with affordability covenants
- Prioritize sites near transit and services

**Expected Impact:**
- 5,000 units on public land
- Zero displacement of existing residents
- Long-term affordability (99-year covenants)

### Action 4: Tenant Protection and Rent Stabilization
**Description:** Strengthen protections for existing tenants

**Implementation:**
- Cap annual rent increases at 3% (currently CPI)
- Expand just cause eviction protections
- Create tenant legal defense fund ($10M)
- Right to counsel for all eviction proceedings

**Expected Impact:**
- 30% reduction in evictions
- Stabilized neighborhoods
- Reduced homelessness

### Action 5: Employer Housing Impact Fee
**Description:** Large employers contribute to affordable housing fund

**Implementation:**
- $20 per square foot fee on new commercial developments
- Apply to companies adding 25+ employees annually
- Estimated $100M annually for affordable housing fund
- Exemptions for non-profits and small businesses

**Expected Impact:**
- Sustainable funding source
- 1,000 units funded annually
- Reduced commute burden

## Metrics for Success

**Housing Production:**
- 10,000+ new affordable units built
- 15,000+ total new units (affordable + market rate)
- 95% occupancy rate in affordable units

**Affordability:**
- Median rent-to-income ratio below 30%
- 50% reduction in rent burden for low-income households
- Eviction rates reduced by 40%

**Equity:**
- Affordable units distributed across all districts
- No net displacement from new development
- 30% of units reserved for extremely low-income (30% AMI)

**Economic:**
- 25,000 construction jobs created
- $500M in economic activity annually
- Increased tax revenue exceeds costs by year 8

## Risk Mitigation

**Construction Delays:**
- Build contingency time into schedules
- Pre-approve modular construction
- Streamline environmental review

**Funding Shortfalls:**
- Diversified revenue sources
- Phased implementation
- Ability to scale based on available funds

**Community Opposition:**
- Extensive public engagement
- Neighborhood planning process
- Design standards for compatibility
- Local hiring requirements

**Legal Challenges:**
- Constitutional review of all provisions
- Precedent from similar policies in other cities
- Legal defense fund
